If you spend time around school-based BCBAs right now, the same theme keeps showing up:

*Everyone wants an FBA. Everyone wants a BIP. Nobody seems to have time to build the system that would make those requests manageable.*

That is not just an internet complaint. It is a real workflow problem in schools.

A teacher is exhausted. An administrator needs a response before the next discipline meeting. A parent is asking what the school is doing. The student has already missed too much instruction. Someone says, "We need a behavior plan."

Then the school BCBA becomes the bottleneck.

Not because the BCBA is slow. Not because the team does not care. The bottleneck happens because many districts have accidentally turned FBA/BIP work into a crisis referral pipeline instead of a tiered behavior support process.

That distinction matters.

## FBA and BIP Work Was Never Supposed to Be a Paperwork Factory

A functional behavior assessment is not a form. It is a problem-solving process.

The U.S. Department of Education describes an FBA as a way to understand why behavior is occurring and what environmental conditions may be connected to it. That information should guide function-based supports and behavior plans that address the conditions contributing to the behavior.

That is the point: the FBA is supposed to connect the team to the variables that matter.

But in schools, the process often gets flattened into paperwork:

- Complete the referral.
- Observe the student.
- Interview staff.
- Write the report.
- Write the BIP.
- Present it at a meeting.
- Move to the next crisis.

That workflow may produce documents, but it does not always produce implementation.

And if the plan is not implemented, monitored, coached, and revised, the FBA/BIP process becomes a high-effort ritual. Everyone can say something was done, but the student and teacher may still be living in the same broken routine.

## Why the Requests Keep Coming

The demand for FBA and BIP support usually grows for three reasons.

### 1. Tier 1 and Tier 2 Systems Are Too Thin

When universal and targeted supports are weak, Tier 3 becomes overcrowded.

The Department of Education's 2024 FBA guidance explicitly connects FBA/BIP work to MTSS and PBIS. Function-based thinking can be used across tiers, not only after a student reaches intensive need.

That is where many districts get stuck. They reserve function-based thinking for the most serious cases, then wonder why the serious cases keep multiplying.

If classrooms do not have predictable routines, high rates of reinforcement, clear correction procedures, and simple data habits, then the BCBA will be pulled into every pattern that escalates.

The problem is not that every student needs a comprehensive FBA.

The problem is that too many adults do not have a usable Tier 1/Tier 2 behavior support pathway before the situation becomes a Tier 3 referral.

### 2. The BCBA Role Is Unclear

In public discussions among school BCBAs, role confusion shows up constantly:

- Is the BCBA supposed to write every FBA?
- Is the BCBA a crisis responder?
- Is the BCBA a coach?
- Is the BCBA responsible for IEP goals?
- Is the BCBA supporting students directly or building adult capacity?

The answer varies wildly by district.

That variation creates workload risk. If every unclear behavior problem becomes "send it to the BCBA," then the role expands until it breaks.

The school BCBA becomes responsible for assessment, intervention writing, classroom coaching, staff training, data review, manifestation meetings, crisis response, parent communication, and district-level behavior systems. Those are different jobs. Some overlap, but they cannot all be treated as unlimited same-day tasks.

Recent workload research in behavior analysis found that behavior analysts and trainees reported high workloads, with indirect responsibilities such as administration, training, collaboration, and supervision taking substantial time. The study also noted that workload can affect service quality, supervision quality, and personal well-being.

That should sound familiar to anyone working in schools.

### 3. Teams Skip the Implementation Question

The most important question in a BIP meeting is often the one nobody wants to ask:

*Can this team actually implement this plan tomorrow at 9:15 a.m.?*

Not in theory. Not with perfect staffing. Not after a two-hour training that nobody has time to attend.

Can they implement it during arrival, math rotations, lunch transition, independent work, and the end-of-day routine when three other students also need support?

Research reviews from the What Works Clearinghouse found that FBA-based interventions can have potentially positive effects on school engagement and problem behavior for students identified with or at risk for emotional disturbance. That is encouraging, but the practical message is not "write more plans."

The practical message is: function-based intervention is worth doing well.

Doing it well means the plan has to survive the classroom.

## A Better Way to Triage FBA/BIP Requests

School BCBAs need a triage system that protects intensive assessment time while still helping teams respond quickly.

Here is the structure I would want in place before every referral becomes a comprehensive FBA.

## Step 1: Separate Urgency From Intensity

Some situations are urgent because adults are stressed. Other situations are intensive because the student needs a high level of individualized support.

Those are not the same thing.

An urgent concern might need same-day coaching, environmental adjustment, a safety check, or administrator support. An intensive concern may need a formal FBA, a function-linked BIP, team training, fidelity checks, and progress monitoring.

If the district treats every urgent problem as an intensive assessment case, the BCBA calendar will collapse.

Use a simple triage question:

**Does this require a comprehensive individualized assessment, or does the team first need a rapid support cycle?**

A rapid support cycle might include:

- defining the behavior in observable terms;
- identifying when and where it is most likely;
- checking whether routines and expectations are clear;
- adding one antecedent support;
- adding one replacement behavior prompt;
- increasing reinforcement for the behavior the team wants;
- reviewing data within one to two weeks.

That does not replace an FBA when an FBA is needed. It prevents the district from using comprehensive assessment as the only doorway to help.

## Step 2: Build a Basic FBA/BIP Pathway

PBIS guidance includes practical FBA training resources for school-based personnel. That matters because the BCBA should not be the only person in the district who can think functionally.

For many schools, the missing layer is a basic FBA/BIP pathway for mild to moderate, predictable behavior patterns.

That pathway should answer:

- Who can facilitate basic problem solving?
- What data are required before referral?
- What classroom supports should be attempted first?
- When does the concern move to comprehensive FBA?
- Who coaches implementation?
- When does the team reconvene?

Without that pathway, everything jumps from "teacher concern" to "BCBA referral."

That is not a system. That is a queue.

## Step 3: Make BIPs Smaller and More Coachable

Many BIPs fail because they are too large for the setting.

A technically beautiful plan can still be a practical disaster if it asks a teacher to track five behaviors, deliver three schedules of reinforcement, run a token system, prompt a replacement response, avoid certain language, collect ABC data, and maintain instruction for the rest of the class.

The better question is:

**What is the smallest plan the team can implement with fidelity that still matches the likely function of behavior?**

Start there.

Then coach. Observe. Adjust. Add complexity only when the team has earned it.

In schools, sustainable implementation beats elegant paperwork.

## Step 4: Protect BCBA Time for the Work Only a BCBA Can Do

This is where school leaders need to be honest.

If the BCBA spends the entire week chasing paperwork and crisis calls, they will not have time to build the system that reduces paperwork and crisis calls.

BCBA time should be protected for:

- complex assessment;
- high-risk or high-intensity behavior support;
- staff coaching on function-based plans;
- district-level training systems;
- data review and decision rules;
- ethical consultation around scope, consent, and implementation capacity.

That does not mean the BCBA refuses to help. It means the district stops using a specialist as the only access point for behavior support.

## What Administrators Should Do This Month

If you supervise or work with a school BCBA, do not start by asking, "How can we get through more FBAs?"

Ask these questions instead:

1. Which referrals could have been prevented with stronger Tier 1 or Tier 2 supports?
2. Which tasks are we assigning to the BCBA that could be handled by a trained team process?
3. Which BIPs are too complex for real classroom implementation?
4. Where do teachers need coaching rather than another document?
5. What data tell us whether our plans are being implemented?

Then build one practical improvement.

Not a district-wide overhaul. One improvement.

Create a referral decision tree. Train one team in basic FBA. Build a 10-day rapid support cycle. Add fidelity checks to BIP reviews. Give the BCBA one protected block each week for coaching instead of paperwork.

Small systems changes compound.

## The Real Goal

The goal is not fewer FBAs because we want less work.

The goal is fewer unnecessary FBAs because schools have stronger behavior systems.

When an FBA is needed, it should get the time and attention it deserves. When a BIP is written, it should be simple enough to implement and strong enough to matter. When a teacher asks for help, the team should have more than one answer.

That is how the school BCBA moves out of the referral bottleneck and back into systems leadership.

And that is how students get support before the crisis becomes the plan.
